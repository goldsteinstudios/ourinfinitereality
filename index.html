Our Infinite Reality

There's a shape that persistent things share. We keep finding it—at the bottom of mathematics, inside ancient philosophy, written in tree rings. It's the same shape every time.

Cut a tree in half. You'll find rings around a center that's often hollow—sometimes completely rotted away. But the tree stands. A hurricane wraps the most violent winds on Earth around an eye of perfect stillness. Try to catch the present moment and it's already gone; try to find the "you" that's looking and you'll chase it forever.

These aren't poetic metaphors for each other. They're instances of the same structure.

The Recursive Structural Model is our name for the pattern. Contrast—opposites must exist for anything to be distinguishable. Rotation—those opposites can't just sit there frozen; they have to orbit each other, stay in motion. Closure—the orbit has to complete, has to come back, or it just spirals away into nothing.

That's it. That's the grammar of persistence. Euler wrote it in five symbols two centuries ago: eiπ + 1 = 0. Ancient Chinese observers compressed it into 81 chapters two millennia before that. Trees write it in wood every year without knowing they're doing it.

We're not proving anything. We're pointing at what's already there.
