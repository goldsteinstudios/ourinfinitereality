Example 1: The Wheel Revolution
三十輻共一轂，當其無，有車之用
"Thirty spokes share one hub; in its nothingness, there is the use of the wheel"
drawing a wheel in the air
Okay, picture a wagon wheel. You've got thirty wooden spokes—solid, strong, tangible. They all converge on… what?
A hole.
The hub is literally a hole. And not just any hole—it's the EXACT RIGHT SIZE hole for the axle. Too big? Wheel wobbles off. Too small? Wheel won't turn. The emptiness has to be precisely maintained.
• 轂 (gū) = hub = the hollow center
• 當其無 (dāng qí wú) = "exactly its nothingness"
• 有車之用 (yǒu chē zhī yòng) = "there is wheel's usefulness"
Now here's what breaks my brain: You can replace every single spoke. You can replace the rim. You can replace everything EXCEPT the hole. The moment you fill the hole, you don't have a broken wheel—you have no wheel at all.
The emptiness isn't just important. It's the ONLY thing that can't be removed.
In our notation: The spokes are the gradient field (G₁), the rim is the surface (P₁), but the hub? That's our O₁—the hollow origin that everything literally revolves around.
Example 2: The Clay Conspiracy
埏埴以為器，當其無，有器之用
"Mold clay to make a vessel; in its nothingness, there is the vessel's use"
miming pottery
You're at a pottery wheel. You've got this lump of undifferentiated clay-Dao—formless potential, not yet anything. And what's the ENTIRE PURPOSE of your work?
To make a distinction that births inside-void and outside-form together.
Watch what actually happens: Before your hands shape the clay, there's no "pot-space" and no "not-pot-space"—just clay-being. The moment the wall forms, BOTH sides appear simultaneously:
• The emptiness inside (無) that will hold tea
• The clay wall and everything beyond it (有)
Neither existed before the boundary. The pot doesn't "surround existing emptiness"—it generates both inside-space and outside-space from undifferentiated kitchen-Dao.
This is wu-ming (無名)—the act of parting wholeness into inside and outside, void and form.
Think about how insane this is: You buy a bowl for the nothing it contains. You pay money for shaped absence that didn't exist until the boundary appeared.
• 埏埴 (shān zhí) = mold clay
• 器 (qì) = vessel/container
And here's the kicker—the better the pot, the more nothing it contains. A master potter isn't someone who uses more clay. They're someone who creates MORE ELEGANT EMPTINESS.
A thick, clunky amateur pot? Mostly clay, little emptiness. A thin, elegant master's work? Mostly emptiness, minimal clay.
We literally measure pottery skill by how much nothing someone can create.
Example 3: The Room Where Nothing Happens
鑿戶牖以為室，當其無，有室之用
"Cut doors and windows to make a room; in its nothingness, there is the room's use"
gesturing around
Look around you right now. You're in a room (or looking at one). What makes it a room?
The walls? No—those just define the boundary. The floor? No—that just provides a surface. The ceiling? No—that just covers.
The room IS THE EMPTINESS that came into being when the walls did. You don't live in walls—you live in the inside-void that the walls generated by making a distinction.
• 鑿 (záo) = cut/chisel out
• 戶牖 (hù yǒu) = doors and windows
• 室 (shì) = room
And check this out—when we cut doors and windows, what are we doing? We're making holes in the walls. We're ADDING MORE NOTHING. We're creating more boundaries that generate more inside/outside distinctions.
An expensive house has bigger rooms (more generated void), larger windows (more inside/outside boundaries), higher ceilings (more vertical void-space). We literally pay premium prices for extra emptiness that springs into being when walls appear!
"This apartment is $3000 a month." "Why so expensive?" "It has lots of nothing that was generated by very precise boundaries!" "SOLD!"
The Punchline That Changes Everything
故有之以為利，無之以為用
"Therefore having-things for benefit, nothing-ness for use"
This is the mic drop:
• 有之以為利 (yǒu zhī yǐ wéi lì) = Things/form/substance provide benefit
• 無之以為用 (wú zhī yǐ wéi yòng) = Nothingness provides FUNCTION
The solid stuff (有) gives you the benefits—the spokes make the wheel strong, the clay makes the bowl holdable, the walls keep the rain out.
But the nothing (無) gives you the ACTUAL FUNCTION—the hole makes it rotate, the emptiness holds water, the space is where you live.
Form provides features. Void provides function.
Wait—Let's Get Precise About What Just Happened
pause, this matters
Here's the thing that's easy to miss: Before you form the pot, there's no "pot-emptiness" waiting to be surrounded. There's just undifferentiated Dao—formless wholeness.
The act of drawing the boundary creates BOTH sides simultaneously:
• Inside-void (無) that can hold tea
• Outside-form (有) that is the clay wall and everything beyond it
Neither the emptiness nor the form existed before the boundary appeared. The pot's wall doesn't "surround existing emptiness"—it parts undifferentiated Dao into inside-space and outside-space.
This is why Chapter 1 says 無名天地之始—"Wu-ming (the act of making distinction) continuously sources the dimensional framework (天地)." Each pot generates its own heaven-earth, its own inside-coordinates and outside-coordinates, its own dimensional gradient.
Before the pot: undifferentiated clay-Dao The moment of forming: wu-ming, the distinction-act After the forming: inside-void (無) and outside-form (有) existing together
Both sides spring into being when the boundary appears. Neither is primary. Both are equally real.
Now, once the boundary exists, we can use simpler language: "the walls hold the void," "the clay maintains the emptiness," "the hub organizes the spokes." That's fine—it's describing what's true after the pot is formed, operationally speaking.
But never forget what happened first: The boundary co-generated both sides from undifferentiated wholeness.
