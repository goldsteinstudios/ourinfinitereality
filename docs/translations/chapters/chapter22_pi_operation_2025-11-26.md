| Tool | Anchor (O) | Radius (G) | Arc (P) | Function |
|------|------------|------------|---------|----------|
| Pole vault | Pole base | Pole length | Body sweeps π | River crossed |
| **Scythe** | Feet planted | Handle+blade | Blade sweeps π | Field harvested |
| Wheel | Axle | Spoke | Rim rotates 2π | Motion without sliding |
| Door | Hinge | Door width | Door sweeps π | Space opened |
| Compass | Pin | Arm length | Pencil sweeps 2π | Circle drawn |
| Well sweep | Fulcrum | Beam length | Bucket sweeps π | Water raised |

**Note on Scythe**: Not "grain cut" (knife logic) but "field harvested" (arc logic). You cannot harvest with a knife—the field is impossible to linear force. The scythe's arc is what makes harvest possible.

Every tool: **O → G → P → function**

Origin. Gradient. Perimeter. Done.

## 反 (fǎn) as 2π Completion

Chapter 40: 反者道之動

If 曲 (curving) is the π-operation (half-turn), then 反 (reversal) is what happens when the arc closes back on itself.

### The Scythe Cycle

```
Standing grain (undifferentiated field)
        ↓
    [ANCHOR planted - O]
        ↓
    [RADIUS established - G]
        ↓
    [ARC swept - π radians]
        ↓
    Grain cut / Space cleared
        ↓
    [STEP FORWARD]
        ↓
    P₁ becomes O₂
        ↓
    [NEXT ARC]
```

Each arc: π radians
Full cycle (out and return): 2π radians

**反 = the return stroke that completes the circle**

### The Pole Vault Sequence

```
Near bank (origin position)
        ↓
    Plant pole (establish O)
        ↓
    Commit body (become radius G)
        ↓
    Release into arc (sweep π)
        ↓
    Far bank (P₁ reached)
        ↓
    [But now you need to return...]
        ↓
    Plant pole again (O₂)
        ↓
    Sweep arc (another π)
        ↓
    Near bank (return)

Total: 2π = complete cycle = 反
```

**反者道之動** = "Reversal is pattern's movement"

The pattern doesn't just go out—it completes the circle. The arc that crosses the river *implies* the arc that returns. Together: 2π.

## 利 (lì) as the π-Operation Applied

### The Scythe-Not-Knife Distinction

**Critical**: 利 documents the SCYTHE operation, not knife-cutting.

You cannot harvest a field with a knife. A knife cuts by linear push—one stalk at a time, exhausting, inadequate to the task. The field wins.

The scythe solves the impossible problem:

```
Farmer stance (O) ─── Handle (G) ───→ Blade
                                        │
                                   [ARC SWEEP π]
                                        │
                                   Grain falls in swath
```

### Character Decomposition

| Component | Meaning | Function |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| 禾 (hé) | Standing grain | The undifferentiated field, infinite potential |
| 刂 (dāo) | Scythe blade | The edge that arcs through substrate |

**利 = scythe-blade sweeping through grain = π-operation applied to field**

The scythe doesn't "cut" in the knife sense. It **arcs**. And in that arc:

1. **Inside** created (cleared, harvested, void)
2. **Outside** created (still standing, uncut, full)
3. **Boundary** traced (where the blade passed)

This is 相生 made physical. One stroke, both poles arise.

## The Door Paradox

The wall is impassable. Solid. You cannot go through.

But attach one edge to a hinge (O), and that same solid surface becomes an *operator*:

```
Wall (closed) ──[rotate π]──> Opening (passage)
Opening      ──[rotate π]──> Wall (closed)

Full cycle: 2π = wall → opening → wall
```

**The door doesn't move through the wall. The door IS the wall, rotating around a fixed point.**

Same material. Same structure. But anchored at one edge, barrier transforms to passage.

This is 為無為:
- The door "acts" (swings)
- The door "doesn't act" (it's still a wall)
- Both true simultaneously, depending on reference frame

## 無為 as the π-Operation

Traditional reading: "non-action," "doing nothing," "effortless effort"

Geometric reading: **engaging the arc instead of the straight-line push**

You still act. You still exert. But you work *with* the geometry:

| Approach | Method | Result |
|----------|--------|--------|
| 有為 (forcing) | Push straight through | Resistance, exhaustion |
| 無為 (arc) | Find anchor, establish radius, release into curve | Completion without fighting |

The pole vaulter doesn't fight the river. They find the fixed point, commit to the radius, and let the arc do the work.

**無為 = the π-operation instead of brute force**
